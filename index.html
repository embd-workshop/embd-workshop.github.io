About

Aligning ML with experiments that matter

Machine learning has transformed biology by advancing models of protein structure, function, gene regulation, molecular interactions, and cellular behavior. Yet a central challenge remains: how can ML models be aligned with experimental reality so they actually accelerate biological discovery?

High-performing models often fail to translate to the bench because experimental validation is expensive, low-throughput, and constrained by distribution shift. Benchmarks frequently reward static accuracy rather than experimental utility, and model outputs may ignore critical chemical, biophysical, or cellular constraints.

The Experimentally-Grounded Machine Learning for Biological Discovery (EMBD) workshop aims to bring together ML researchers, computational biologists, chemists, and experimental scientists to define shared standards, benchmarks, and workflows for ML systems that produce hypotheses researchers can trust and validate.

Expensive experimental validation

Thousands of computational candidates may be generated, but prioritizing the few that can be tested experimentally is the core bottleneck.

Benchmarks miss wet-lab utility

Strong in-silico results often fail to predict experimental success because metrics ignore physical viability, robustness, and distribution shift.

Biological and chemical alignment

Models and objectives must embed biological, chemical, and experimental constraints such as solubility, yield, toxicity, and cellular context.

Competition

A wet-lab validated competition track

Teams will design inhibitors for β-lactamases, a primary driver of antibiotic resistance, with top candidates validated in the lab.

The competition is built around experimentally grounded discovery: participants develop ML approaches that propose molecules with real biochemical and structural plausibility.

Winning designs will be selected for experimental validation by the lab of organizer Ariane Mora, which will test the top ∼10 in-silico performers.

Results will be presented at the workshop, and top teams will have the opportunity to contribute to a high-impact biological publication.

Themes

EMBD technical focus areas

We seek contributions that combine machine learning, wet-lab awareness, and biologically grounded evaluation for discovery-driven systems.

01

Experimental design

Methods that nominate actionable hypotheses, quantify uncertainty under distribution shift, and learn from both failed and successful experiments.

02

Benchmarks and metrics

Evaluation frameworks that measure biological utility, robustness, and generalization instead of only static predictive accuracy.

03

Biophysical and chemical alignment

Architectures, objectives, and filtering procedures that embed biological, physical, and chemical structure for experimental relevance.

04

Discovery workflows

Closed-loop and agentic systems that connect model generation with experimental feedback and laboratory constraints.

Call for Papers

Submit your work

We invite contributions on experimentally grounded ML for biological discovery, including research, datasets, benchmarks, and experiments.

  • 4–8 pages, plus unlimited references and appendix.
  • Non-archival. Submission does not preclude future publication elsewhere.
  • Double-blind review. Each submission receives at least three reviews.
  • Three tracks: ML Research, Experimental, and Dataset & Benchmark.
  • Submissions due Aug 29, 2026; notifications by Sep 29, 2026.

Submissions will be handled through OpenReview. The portal link will be posted here once available.

Community

Engagement and impact

EMBD is designed to foster collaboration between ML researchers, experimental scientists, and benchmark builders.

The workshop will include spotlight talks for selected submissions, broad poster participation for every accepted paper, and a panel discussion that brings experimental and ML perspectives together.

Our community focus includes a wet-lab validated competition, a best poster award to encourage discussion, and a networking social to help participants connect across disciplines.
